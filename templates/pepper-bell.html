Introduction

Pepper Bell is a warm-season crop that is widely consumed fresh, with a significant portion of production being used for processed products such as pickling and freezing. Pepper Bell is one of the most popular sweet pepper varieties globally.

Care

Pepper Bell can be cultivated using various methods, including direct seeding, transplanting, and container gardening. When planting, seedlings should be spaced 12-18 inches apart to ensure proper air circulation and sunlight penetration. Pepper Bell plants require full sun and consistent moisture, especially when fruiting. Regular fertilization and pruning are essential for promoting healthy growth and maximizing yields. Support systems, such as cages or trellises, can help to keep plants upright and encourage better fruiting.



Soil

Well-draining, fertile soils with a pH of 6.0-6.8 are ideal for Pepper Bell cultivation. Soils should be rich in organic matter and have a moderate level of nutrients. Mulching around plants can help to retain soil moisture, suppress weeds, and regulate soil temperature.

Climate

Pepper Bell is a warm-season crop that thrives in temperatures between 18 and 32C. They can be grown in a variety of climates, but optimal growth occurs in regions with mild winters and long, warm summers. Adequate moisture, especially during the fruiting stage, is essential for producing high-quality peppers. Windy conditions can cause damage to plants, while dry, sunny conditions can lead to sunscald on fruits.

Pepper-Bell Diseases

Pepper Bell Bacterial Spot

Bacterial spot occurs everywhere in the world where pepper bells are grown and results in significant losses. It is most serious in areas that have warm, humid weather and may not be economically important in cool and/or dry climates.

Bacterial spot is caused by a bacterium that infects both leaves and fruit.
Infected fruit develop small, circular, and raised lesions that are typically brown or black in color.
These lesions can lead to fruit decay and render the pepper bells unfit for eating.
Infected leaves have small, dark spots that can lead to defoliation.
Leaf loss weakens the plant when it occurs many years in a row, making it more susceptible to other diseases and pests.
