Cercospora Leaf Blight Soybean

Marssonina fragariae

Fungus

In a Nutshell

  • Reddish-purple or bronze discoloration on leaves.
  • Premature defoliation.
  • Reduced yields and seed quality.
  • Weakened plants more susceptible to other diseases and pests.

Symptoms

The first visible symptoms of Cercospora Leaf Blight in soybean appear as small, circular, grayish-brown spots on leaves, often accompanied by yellowing of the surrounding tissue. As the disease progresses, the affected leaves turn yellow, wilt, and fall off, leading to reduced yields and quality. The weakened plants become more susceptible to other diseases and pests.

Recommendations

Organic Control

  • Remove and destroy infected leaves and fruits to prevent the spread of the disease
  • Improve air circulation around the plants by pruning and spacing them properly.
  • Water plants in the morning or evening to reduce humidity and prevent water from collecting on the leaves.
  • Apply a mulch around the plants to retain moisture and suppress weeds.
  • Use organic fungicides containing copper or sulfur as a last resort.


Chemical Control

  • Always consider an integrated approach with preventive measures together with biological treatments if available.
  • Protectant fungicides such as azoxystrobin, tebuconazole, or captan can be used to prevent the disease.
  • Once Cercospora Leaf Blight has been detected, fungicides based on difenoconazole, myclobutanil, or sulphur can be used to control the development of the fungus.
  • Ensure Cercospora Leaf Blight fungicides from different chemical groups are used to avoid the development of resistance.


Select and apply ONLY ONE of the following products to your crops:

  • Azoxystrobin 11.0% SC
  • Captan 50.0% WP
  • Tebuconazole 18.3% SC
  • Difenoconazole 25.0% EC
  • Myclobutanil 10.0% WP
  • Sulphur 80.0% WDG


What caused it?

Cercospora Leaf Blight is a disease caused by the fungus Cercospora kikuchii. It survives the winter on infected leaves and debris. In the spring, the fungus resumes growth and starts to produce spores, which are dispersed by wind and water. These spores land on developing leaves and start a new infection. Humid environments and wetting periods of leaves are essential for the infection.

Preventive Measures

  • Use tolerant or resistant varieties
  • Monitor orchards for signs of the disease.
  • Remove and dispose of infected leaves and debris.
  • Improve air circulation around the plants by pruning and spacing them properly.
  • Water plants in the morning or evening to reduce humidity and prevent water from collecting on the leaves.
  • Apply a mulch around the plants to retain moisture and suppress weeds.
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization, which can promote lush growth and increase the risk of disease.
