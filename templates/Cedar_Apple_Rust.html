Apple Scab

Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae

Fungus

In a Nutshell

  • Orange, gelatinous spots on the undersides of leaves
  • Yellow-orange spots on the upper surfaces of leaves
  • Brown, corky lesions on fruits
  • Defoliation and reduced fruit yield

Symptoms

The first visible symptoms of Cedar Apple Rust appear in the spring as orange, gelatinous spots on the undersides of leaves. These spots are often accompanied by yellow-orange spots on the upper surfaces of leaves. As the disease progresses, the spots enlarge and become more numerous, leading to defoliation and reduced fruit yield. On the fruits, brown, corky lesions appear, which can reduce the storage capacity and the quality.

Recommendations

Organic Control

  • Remove and destroy infected leaves and fruits to prevent the spread of the disease
  • Improve air circulation by pruning trees to allow for more space between branches
  • Water trees in the morning to allow for drying during the day, and avoid overhead irrigation
  • Consider using organic fungicides such as neem oil or horticultural oil


Chemical Control

  • Always consider an integrated approach with preventive measures together with biological treatments if available
  • Protectant fungicides such as mancozeb or chlorothalonil can be sprayed around bud break to avoid the disease
  • Once Cedar Apple Rust has been detected, fungicides based on myclobutanil or sulphur can be used to control the development of the fungus
  • Ensure Cedar Apple Rust fungicides from different chemical groups are used to avoid the development of resistance


Select and apply ONLY ONE of the following products to your crops:

  • Chlorothalonil 75.0% WP
  • Mancozeb 75.0% WP
  • Myclobutanil 10.0% WP
  • Sulphur 80.0% WDG


What caused it?

Cedar Apple Rust is a disease caused by the fungus Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae. It requires two hosts to complete its life cycle: apple trees and juniper trees. The fungus overwinters on juniper trees, producing gelatinous, orange structures called telial horns in the spring. These structures release spores that are carried by the wind to apple trees, where they infect leaves and fruits. The fungus then produces spores that are carried back to juniper trees, completing the life cycle.

Preventive Measures

  • Use tolerant or resistant varieties
  • Monitor orchards for signs of the disease.
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves and fruits.
  • Improve air circulation by pruning trees.
  • Water trees in the morning and avoid overhead irrigation.
  • Consider applying a fungicide in the dormant season to prevent the disease.
  • Remove or prune juniper trees in the vicinity of apple trees to reduce the risk of infection.
